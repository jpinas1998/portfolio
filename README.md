# Backend WOD

### My career, except you have to walk through it.

**Backend WOD** is the interactive portfolio of [Joaquín Pina Socorro](https://www.linkedin.com/in/jpinas1998-software-engineer/), a development team lead and backend engineer specialized in Python.

Instead of asking you to inspect another PDF full of progress bars claiming someone is “92% proficient in PostgreSQL”, this portfolio turns my professional history into a small pixel-art game set inside a CrossFit box. Each workout is a chapter of my career. Each station is a real project, technical challenge or regrettably character-building production experience.

No cover letter was harmed during development. Several weekends were.

## What is this, exactly?

You control a tiny, disproportionately athletic version of me and move through five stages of my career: backend engineering, team leadership, cloud infrastructure, data platforms, product modernization, teaching and public-impact software.

Walk close to a station and the game reveals what I worked on, what I shipped and the results behind it. To advance, you must complete the movement shown at the gate: double-unders, snatches, burpees or box jumps. This is still less exhausting than explaining microservices to a committee.

The experience covers work such as:

- Leading product development from field requirements to production.
- Building APIs, RBAC systems and data-heavy platforms with Python and Django REST Framework.
- Designing reporting, document-processing and automation systems on AWS and GCP.
- Modernizing legacy products without performing the traditional ritual of rewriting everything and discovering six months later why the old code looked like that.
- Shipping software used in logistics, energy, carbon management, healthcare and public services.

## How to play

- Use **←** and **→** to move.
- Use **↑** or **Space** to jump.
- Stand beside a gate and perform the movement it requests:
  - **D** — double-unders
  - **S** — snatch
  - **B** — burpee
  - **J** — box jump
- On touch devices, use the on-screen controls and double-tap the striped gate.
- Reach the end alive. Emotionally is optional.

There is also a hidden reward after the final workout, because apparently finishing a portfolio was not enough scope for one project.

## Why a game?

A CV tells you where someone worked. It rarely shows how they think, what they care about or whether they will spend an unreasonable amount of time polishing a tiny interaction nobody explicitly requested.

This project is meant to do all three.

The CrossFit theme is not a random skin pasted over a résumé. It mirrors how I approach engineering: break a large problem into movements, keep the fundamentals solid, measure the result and finish the work even when the last 10% has begun feeding on your soul.

## Behind the pixels

The portfolio is built with **React, TypeScript and Vite**, with custom pixel-art components, keyboard and touch controls, procedural sound effects, responsive layouts, accessibility considerations and Vercel analytics.

There is no backend. I build those for a living, so for once I allowed myself not to provision a database just to display my own name.

## The person outside the sprite

I am a development team lead and backend software engineer with more than five years of experience building and modernizing production systems. My main territory is Python, Django REST Framework, PostgreSQL, MongoDB, ClickHouse, AWS, GCP, Docker and CI/CD — plus the less marketable skill of remaining calm while a deployment develops free will.

If the portfolio did its job, you now know more about my work than a conventional résumé would tell you. If it did not, at least you made a pixel engineer do burpees.
